## Introduction

Human respiratory influenza is one of the most greatly recognized seasonal infections that is known for causing severe epidemics and pandemics, both historically and today (Zambon, 1999, Linster et al., 2019).  Although several types of influenza exist, one of the most well-researched ones is influenza A and its viruses (IAVs), due to its ability to undergo antigenic evolution, leading to higher transmissibility (Linster et al., 2019).  Two particular glycoproteins of the influenza A virus, hemagglutinin (HA) and neuraminidase (NA) are usually recognized as key determinants of its pathogenicity (Linster et al., 2019, Byrd-Leotis et al., 2017).  Their functions are mainly responsible for viral entry and infection by fusion with the endosomal membrane and cleavage activation, predominantly with cells in the upper respiratory tract and nasal passages (Byrd-Leotis et al., 2017).  For these reasons, the different types of immune cells involved during infection have often been the topic of many research studies to further understand the immune response at the cellular level.  As a result, many animal models have typically been used to study respiratory influenza to test the potential of possible therapeutic drugs or vaccines to support immune function and alleviate the infection, such as ferrets, swine, and guinea pigs (Nguyen et al., 2021).  However, out of all of the animal models, the most commonly used model has been the mouse/murine model, due to their convenience in most clinical studies, along with the symptoms they develop that can be more easily traced as markers for disease pathogenesis (Nguyen et al., 2021).  However, using mouse models does often have its share of limitations, as their immune systems and transmissibility patterns of influenza A vary greatly and thus cannot be translated completely accurately to humans (Nguyen et al., 2021).  One such noteworthy study, for example, which was conducted by Kazer et al. in 2024, has even previously applied mouse models to piece together a full cell atlas, to illustrate the process of a respiratory influenza infection and the immune response in the nasal mucosa, with single cell RNA-sequencing technology.

Single cell RNA-sequencing (scRNA-seq) has often been at the heart of understanding many respiratory infections, especially influenza (Medaglia et al., 2022).  It has often been used to investigate elements of host pathogenicity, treatment, and immune response at the cellular level, such as combination drug therapy, innate immunity or transcriptional markers in various cell types indicative of disease progression (Medaglia et al., 2022, Zhang et al., 2023, Kelly et al., 2022).  One of the most widely used programs that has been used to conduct many different types of scRNA-seq analyses is the *Seurat* package in R (Satija et al., 2015).  The *Seurat* package is able to incoroporate a wide range of different techniques, such as *in situ* hybridizations, that allow it to effectively determine spatial locations of cells and identifying landmark genes, allowing it to piece together different clusters according to different cell types (Satija et al., 2015).  When it comes to the visualization of these clusters, the most prominent method that has been greatly accepted today is the Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) (Becht et al., 2018).  UMAP embedding has typically been most useful in illustrating high-dimensional data for cell clusters in a low-dimensional space for many scRNA-seq analyses, being more robust at creating highly reproducible visualizations that take less computing time (Becht et al., 2018, Xiang et al., 2021).  However, the most noteworthy aspect about this method that has made it more valuable than prior visualization methods, most notably, t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (t-SNE), is its capability of determining cell clusters that best reflect and maintain the global cell populations from the samples and/or organisms they were obtained from (Becht et al., 2018, Xiang et al., 2021).  Unfortunately, one key limitation that remains in this environment of low-dimensional analysis of high-dimensional data, both in Seurat and UMAP, is that embedding these clusters requires cell data to be log-normalized and scaled, which can often underestimate the nature of the cell populations that raw unfiltered, nonlinear counts provide (Verma & Engelhardt, 2020).  For annotation, while Seurat has the capability to annotate cell types, it is still limited at its prediction ability for cell types that may be similar to or rarer than others (Huang et al., 2020).  Other tools, however, such as SingleR, have shown similar success at annotation with high accuracy, while being less weighed down by the limitations present in Seurat (Huang et al., 2020).  Considering that differential expression also plays a major role in scRNA-seq, there have also been different types of DE software that have proven effective for understanding changes in gene expression when studying cell clusters, including those normally designed for bulk transcriptomics such as DESeq2 (Wang et al., 2019).  As a result, DESeq2 and other DE software have been instrumental for understanding how the expression of certain genes are essential for regulating cell types pathways that are pertinent to the immune response, such as B cells and macrophages (Cembellin-Prieto et al., 2025, Pollenus et al., 2019).  Lastly, in functional annotation, Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA), when paired with Gene Ontology (GO), has been shown to be useful for understanding upregulation and downregulation patterns in immune cells (Li et al., 2025).  Such analyses can reveal important information for understanding how this can affect immunity to disease as a whole, especially if dysregulation occurs that could otherwise affect important biological functions, such as in T cells or monocytes (Li et al., 2025).

With scRNA-seq being a major focal point of understanding respiratory influenza, it comes as no surprise that it can be applied to understand the different layers of immune activity during pathogenesis of a disease (Kazer et al., 2024).  Henceforth, expanding from this study by Kazer et al., the goal of this analysis was to explore the different types of cells and their functions that can be observed in mouse models during infection, and apply single-cell RNA sequencing to understand some of the key genes and features that help to identify some of the most dominant cell populations that facilitate the immune response (2024).  In addition, this analysis also sought to investigate how these are reflected in expression changes across different tissues and timepoints of respiratory infections, especially influenza A (Kazer et al., 2024).


## Methods
Raw single-cell RNA sequencing data was obtained and adopted from the study conducted by Kazer et al. in 2024 and transformed into a Seurat object to import into R (v4.4.3) (2026).  In this Seurat object, mice had been infected with the Influenza A virus, and a total of 156,572 cells were collected in the nasal mucosa found in the upper respiratory tract (Kazer et al., 2024).  Over a post-infection period of about 14 days, cells were collected during five specific timepoints, at Day 0 (Naive), Day 2 (D02), Day 5 (D05), Day 8 (D08), and Day 14 (D14) (Kazer et al., 2024).  In addition, during these timepoints, the cells were also collected from across three different types of tissue in the nasal mucosa, which were the respiratory mucosa (RM), olfactory mucosa (OM), and lateral nasal gland (LM) (Kazer et al., 2024).  Once collected, the data was pre-processed, and a Seurat object was created using the *Seurat* package in R (v.4.4.3) (Satija et al., 2015, Hao et al., 2023).

All clusters were then automatically annotated for their most dominant cell types by using the *SingleR* package (v2.8.0), both at the cell level and the cluster level (Aran et al., 2019).  As a reference dataset, the MouseRNAseqData was downloaded and used from the *celldex* package (v1.16.0), adopted from an epigenomic and transcriptomic mice study conducted by Benayoun et al. (2019, Aran et al., 2019).  First, to explore all the different cell types across the entire cell community, the normalized RNA assay was extracted from the UMAP Seurat object and ran through SingleR.  Then, for cluster level annotation, the normalized RNA assays of the UMAP Seurat object were pseudobulked and used in SingleR.  Both levels of annotation were then visualized using separate annotated UMAPs of the cells and clusters.  Finally, to verify the accuracy of these annotations by SingleR using manual annotation, one cluster was selected, and its top three gene markers were used to conduct a literature search.

To determine the genes of interest, which were defined as the top significant gene markers for each annotated cell type, the *FindAllMarkers* function in the *Seurat* package was first used on the clustered cells to obtain all gene markers for each cluster as a starting directory (Satija et al., 2015, Hao et al., 2023).  This data was then filtered to include only the top gene marker per cluster, with the following parameters: an average log2Fold change greater than 0.5 (*avg_log2FC* > 0.5), an adjusted p-value less than 0.05 (*p_val_adj* < 0.05), at least 25% coverage of cells in the cluster (*pct.1* > 0.25), and no more than 10% coverage of cells in other clusters (*pct.2* < 0.10).  These parameters were selected to facilitate more discovery of important and distinct gene markers, while still finding those that were more unique to specific cell types, as multiple stricter parameters, such as pct.1, often limited the number of top genes per cluster or resulted in a cluster not reporting any genes.  Once these top gene markers were identified per cluster, this list was then further filtered to identify the topmost gene for each cell type, given that each annotation was still represented by multiple clusters and thus multiple top genes, whilst still retaining the same parameters.  The expression patterns of these top genes were then visualized and examined using UMAP embedding as feature plots to justify that these markers were unique to each of their respective cell types.

Finally, to understand how gene expression differed between tissue types in the nasal mucosa, as well as how it differed across different timepoints, both before infection and post-infection, differential expression analysis was carried out on one selected cluster using the DESeq2 (v1.46.2) package (Love et al., 2014).  Pairwise comparisons were sampled from the results and analyzed further to compare how their gene expressions changed with respect to infection time and tissue type.  One pairwise comparison was sampled for exploring time and disease ontology each, while two were sampled for investigating tissue types, in order to investigate how the olfactory mucosa (OM) and respiratory (RM) each differed from the lateral nasal gland (LNG), which was used as a reference.  Each time a comparison was sampled to investigate the effect of a specific variable, other variables affecting expression were kept constant, such as timepoint or tissue type.  Visualizations of each comparison were done using heat maps, with shrinkage being applied to log2FoldChange values using the *ashr* (v2.2-6.3) shrinkage method (Stephens, 2017).  In each heat map, the genes were ordered based on decreasing log2FoldChange values.  Lastly, functional annotation of each sample pairwise comparison was carried out using the *org.Mm.eng.db* (v3.20.0) database, the *clusterProfiler* (v4.14.6), and *enrichplot* (v1.26.6) packages ().  Gene Ontology (GO)-enriched Fast Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (FGSEA) was applied using Biological Process (BP) ontology, to discover which functions tied to immune functions were upregulated and downregulated in different tissues and over the infection time period (Ashburner et al., 2000, The Gene Ontology Consortium, 2025, Thomas et al., 2021, Korotkevich et al., 2016).  The GO-GSEA results for each comparison were then visualized using dot plots and validated further with ridge plots.

## Results
### Dimensionality Reduction and Initial Clustering of Mouse Cells
Dimensionality reduction of mouse cell data from the Seurat object was carried out using PC analysis, to identify relevant PCs to retain for clustering.  UMAP embedding was then used to visualize all of the distinct clusters and their spatial separation with the applied PCs.  When looking at the elbow plot in Figure 1 after applying log-normalization and scaling, a total 20 PCs were identified in total from the data.  However, the variance in cells appeared to decrease at around 15 PCs, as evident by the elbow point levelling off at around PC15.  As such, up to 15 PCs were applied to further downstream analysis for UMAP embedding.

Looking at the initial UMAP for the mouse cells (Figure 2), a total of 37 distinct clusters were identified.  These clusters appeared mostly heterogenous from each other, with certain clusters appearing to form larger groups than others, while still remaining distinct between each other.  Overlap between clusters did appear to be present within the clusters upon closer inspection, but these instances were usually minor and, for the most part, did not have a major impact on heterogeneity.  Cluster sizes were also varied, though certain groups appeared to have similar sizes between each other, only differing by a few tens or hundreds of cells. ### Annotation of Mouse Cell Clusters
To identify cell type/identity for each cluster, all cells and clusters were annotated using SingleR (v2.8.0).  Looking at the annotated UMAP embedding at the cell level (Figure 3), a total of 18 different cell types were identified from the entire cell community of 156,572 cells.  These cell types matched accordingly with the cell types determined in the reference dataset (Benayoun et al., 2019).

Then, looking at the annotated UMAP embedding at the cluster level (Figure 4), which looked more at the more dominant cell types across clusters, this list was consequently reduced to 10 different cell types overall.  Neurons were the most prominent type of cell, representing about 32% of the cell community, with a total frequency of 49,658 cells identified across 5 clusters.  Meanwhile, fibroblasts were the second most prominent cell type, representing about 28% of the cell community, with a total of 27,829 cells spread across 8 clusters, the most number of clusters out of all the categories.  Lastly, the least prominent cell type amongst the 10 dominant ones were T cells, only representing about 0.2% of the community, only being represented by cluster 36, which, again, had a total frequency of 245 cells.

To check the accuracy of the cluster level automatic annotations, cluster 4, which identified its dominant cell type as B cells, was selected for verification using manual annotation with the scientific literature.  The top three significant gene markers identified for this cluster, based on average log2Fold changes and adjusted p values, were *Iglc2* (avg_log2FC = 8.88), *Fcer2a* (avg_log2FC = 8.60), and *Iglc1* (avg_log2FC = 8.56).  The true adjusted p-values for each gene were much smaller and drastically approached 0.  Two research studies, one by Gibson et al. in 2023, which took a closer look at the immunoglobin lambda chain in B cells, and another by Huang et al. in 2024, which investigated the *Fcer2a* gene more in depth in B cells, were, in turn, able to confirm and justify the identified cell type for this cluster as B cells.

### Genes of Interest
After annotating each cluster with SingleR, the top significant gene marker was determined for each of the dominant cell types based on the average log2Fold change and adjusted p-values.  Using UMAP embedding, these genes of interest were then visualized using feature plots, separating them into two groups based on genes belonging to immune cell types (Figure 4) and other cell types belonging to more structural/specialized functions (Figure 5).  The average log2Fold changes ranged from about 4.65, which was exhibited by the *Nqo1* gene in neurons, to 11.54, which was exhibited by the *Gpihbp1* gene in endothelial cells.  Meanwhile, the adjusted p-values for all top gene markers were much smaller that they all greatly approached 0.  Looking at the feature plots, each gene appeared to show a distinct expression pattern, being highly expressed in individual clusters that differed from each other, without major overlap between the dominant cell types.  This helped to further validate the annotations of cell type identities, with each category being supported by at least one uniquely expressed gene marker.

### Differential Expression of a Cell Cluster
Differential expression of cluster 4, identified as B cells, was conducted using DESeq2, in order to investigate and analyze further differences in gene expression based on timepoints of infection, tissue sample, and disease ontology.  This was done by sampling pairwise comparisons of different groups while affecting only a single factor each time, while the rest were kept constant.  However, it was worth noting that the adjusted p-values for these genes were not greater than the cutoff value of 0.05, and thus, it was not possible to evaluate true statistical significance with certainty.  The time variable looked at changes in gene expression between the two furthest timepoints of post-infection (D02 and D14); tissue samples compared differences in each of the oral and respiratory mucosa in relation to the lateral nasal gland, and disease ontology focused on a comparison in gene expression between normal and immediate post-infection conditions.  Starting with the time variable (Figure 7), *Clstn1* was reported to have the greatest log2FoldChange between groups during the post-infection period.  Next, when looking at the comparisons between tissue types, for the olfactory mucosa (OM) in Figure 7, the *Elavl3* gene reportedly had the greatest log2FoldChange and supposed upregulation.  For the respiratory mucosa (RM) in Figure 8, interestingly, the *Gm6619* gene exhibited a high log2FoldChange in the LNG, potentially suggesting its strong upregulation, but again, not with absolute certainty or significance.  Finally, when looking at disease ontology (Figure 9), the period leading up to initial infection, the *Scn9a* gene was identified as having the greatest log2FoldChange before influenza, though its upregulation seemed less apparent as opposed to the other genes shown.

### GO-GSEA Functional Annotation of a Cell Cluster
For the final part of this scRNA-seq analysis, the genes of cluster 4 were then functionally annotated by applying Gene Ontology according to Biological Process (GO-BP) and compared with Fast Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (FGSEA) using dot plots, which were then further justified with ridge plots.  Looking first at the time analysis illustrated by Figures 10 and 11, two of the most significantly upregulated functions, as infection progressed, appeared to be ribonucleoprotein complex biogenesis and MHC class II protein complex assembly, while one of the most signifcantly downregulated functions was the ubiquitin-dependent protein catabolic process.  Then, when comparing the OM and RM mucosal regions to the LNG in Figures 12 and 13, there were some notable differences in expression patterns depending on the tissue type.  In the OM, lymphocyte differentiation appeared to be most significantly upregulated.  However, in contrast, there appeared to be many functions that were downregulated to a similar extent, with one example being sensory perception of chemical stimuli.  In the RM, cytoplasmic translation was one of the most upregulated functions, while the ribose phosphate metabolic process was downregulated.  Lastly, Figures 14 and 15 revealed that, prior to infection, the most significantly upregulated function was translation, and similar to the time analysis, the ubiquitin-dependent protein catabolic process was mostly downregulated.

## Discussion
This single cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) analysis aimed to dive deeper into our understanding of how respiratory disease, namely from influenza A viruses, affected the different cell cluster types and expression patterns of important genes in the nasal mucosa, as obtained from the dataset of mouse models. It was able to investigate a variety of different aspects to describe the identities of cell populations and their uniquely expressed gene markers, though the degree of interpretation for each aspect varies.

Starting with the results observed from the UMAP clusters, there were initially 37 clusters identified from a total of 156,572 cells.  After annotation, there were 10 dominant cell types that were identified from these clusters over the course of post-infection.  Out of these cell types, neurons had the largest presence across the cell populations in multiple clusters.  While explanations for the exact key role of neurons in influenza responses remain unclear in the scientific literature, it is possible that, as an alternative explanation, this result could be indicative of neuro-immune interaction mechanisms or crosstalk (Bin et al., 2023, Chen et al., 2025).  Although these mechanisms are not as well understood, it is possible that they could serve as an underlying explanation for understanding how neurons are more functionally involved in the symptomatic response to influenza after infection (Bin et al., 2023, Chen et al., 2025).  However, T cells, in contrast, had the smallest presence, limited to a single cluster.  This result was similar to that of another study by Chen et al., who similarly observed a decrease in T cells after H1N1 influenza infection but an increase in macrophages (2024).  Here, their justification looked further into how these changed over the course of the infection, along with the effects of bystander T cells, which could suggest, but not for certain, that the immune response could have been more dominated by immune cells involved in a more immediate response (Chen et al., 2024).  When selecting cluster 4, identified as B cells, to annotate manually, to check the performance of SingleR, using *Iglc2*, *Fcer2a*, and *Iglc1*, as the top gene markers according to average log2Fold change, Gibson et al. investigated genetic variation and population diversity arising from the immunoglobin lambda chain locus, which would have included genes encoding for these light chains such as *Iglc1* and *Iglc2*, whereas Huang et al. confirmed the presence and expression of the *Fcer2a* gene (2023, 2024).  Light chains, including those produced by *Iglc1* and *Iglc2*, are incredibly instrumental in immune responses and especially play an essential role against influenza A, even if they are unable to directly bind to the glycoproteins responsible for the pathogenesis, such as hemagglutinin (Xiao et al., 2019).  Instead, as Xiao et al. found in their study, these light chains may affect the conformation of heavy chains, which in turn, modulates their ability to bind to hemagglutinin in response (2019).  Meanwhile, the *FCER2* gene is known for encoding CD23, a particular receptor expressed on B cells that is responsible for the production of IgE antibodies (Chan et al., 2014).  In turn, IgE has been found to be responsible for increasing responsiveness in the respiratory tract during influenza A, in response to airway resistance, which may have been exacerbated by atopic symptoms or stress caused by the virus (Suzuki et al., 1998).

Taking a closer look back at the feature plots for the genes of interest, the results showed that the expression signature of all 10 top gene markers, per cell type, were distinctly separated from each other in different clusters, justifying their identities as unique markers.  In particular, two of these genes stood out the most when comparing the average log2Fold change of the top markers.  Interestingly, the *Gpihbp1* gene in endothelial cells was the one that experienced the greatest average log2Fold change.  Given its expression in more specialized endothelial cells, the connection between this gene and influenza may not be as clearly or directly antiviral.  Nonetheless, when thinking about its function as a transporter of lipoprotein lipase (LPL) into the capillaries, there may be a potential explanation to justify its relevance in the immune response (Davies et al., 2010).  When considering the study conducted by Yin et al., in a glucose-deficient environment, LPL can actually be essential for breaking down and releasing fatty acids in blood vessels for immune cells, such as macrophages, as a possible alternative energy source, especially for necessary and demanding immune processes such as phagocytosis (1997).  Meanwhile, while neurons had the highest cell frequency, its top gene marker, the *Nqo1* gene, experienced the smallest average log2Fold change.  Nonetheless, this lower value could actually be more suggestive of its role as a somewhat more highly conserved gene (Ross & Siegel, 2021, Lacher et al., 2015).  On its own, *Nqo1* plays a key role in regulating redox processes for cells undergoing oxidative stress (Ross & Siegel, 2021).  As part of the *Nrf2* pathway, though, where it is often considered a key target, its importance becomes more apparent as a response to oxidative stress, being much more inducible under the mediation of *Nrf2* (Lacher et al., 2015).  Tying it together, the Nrf2 pathway itself has major implications in protecting most epithelial cells, often the main target of damage caused by IAV-induced oxidative stress (Kosmider et al., 2012).  Hence, while the *Nqo1* gene may be more highly expressed in neurons, which differs from its more indirect target, its more conserved role ensures that essential regions and tissues of the respiratory tract do not succumb to heavy oxidative stress as a result of the IAVs (Kosmider et al., 2012).

Then, with differential gene expression, looking closer at a cluster of B cells, the analysis revealed how different genes and biological functions were either upregulated or downregulated across different tissue regions in the upper respiratory tract, as well as during different stages of influenza pathogenesis, both before and during infection.  Taking a look back at the DE analysis, three different upregulated genes were revealed, though, again, it was not possible to clearly determine their true statistical significance.  *Clstn1* appeared to experience changes in expression over the course of infection; *Elavl3* and *Gm6619* seemed to be mostly influenced by tissue region; and *Scn9a* appeared to experience more expression during pre-infection.  The connection between *Clstn1* and respiratory infection is usually not a sound one, its role as an endosome trafficker may offer more clues about its relevance (Ponomareva et al., 2014).  Given that endosomal system often has a well established role in both innate and adaptive immunity, it is possible that, under a prolonged infection, this gene may have a role in mobilizing these endosomes in response to more pathogens (Gleeson, 2014).  Unfortunately, the literature on *Gm6619* was highly scarce in its exact understanding in the RM.  *Elavl3*, however, though also not as well-researched, has been somewhat established in the olfactory epithelium, likely justifying its presence in the OM (Miller et al., 2016).  The *Scn9a* gene having higher expression pre-infection aligns with the understanding of its function to promote Nav1.7 voltage-gated sodium channels (Diss et al., 2008).  In turn, these Nav1.7 channels, among others like its kind, are important in airway defense, which likely points towards a primary defense against most airborne pathogens (Kocmalova et al., 2016).

Finally, when applying functional annotation to the pairwise comparisons using GO-GSEA, there were some noteworthy biological processes that were essential to immune function, including MHC class II protein complex assembly during late post-infection in the LNG and lymphocyte differentiation in the OM.  Taking a closer look, major histocompatibility (MHC) proteins are known for facilitating adaptive immunity by the recognition of CD4+ T cells, which is usually understood as more of a long-term response after infection (Wieczorek, 2017).  Its interactions during peptide exchange are most interesting, with polymorphic substitutions often creating more variation in alleles and binding preferences to shape how protein complexes can, in turn, respond physiologically to a wider range of infections (Wieczorek, 2017).  Meanwhile, in the OM, the lymphocyte differentiation that occurs in the nasal mucosa is seen as one that provides some of the most crucial protection against airborne antigens and hazards, which may sometimes include an inflammation response (Tracey & Moseman, 2026).  Without this defense, the danger of neuroinvasion, especially by viruses aiming to infect the central nervous system (CNS), hence the reason for this essential response (Tracey & Moseman, 2026).  Most interestingly, though, the ubiquitin-dependent protein catabolic process experienced downregulation in the LNG in comparison to both pre-infection and late post-infection periods, establishing its key role in the earliest stages of infection.  The ubiquitin system is often highly essential in host-pathogen interactions, especially when discussing the innate immunity response, which aligns with early stages of infection (Li et al., 2016).  In fact, because of the process of ubiquination as a key defense against infection, studies have revealed how immediate pathogenic invasion has even evolved to interfere with these signalling pathways, making them more potent, a key area of research often under continuous investigation (Mukherjee & Dikic, 2022).

In conclusion, this single cell RNA-sequencing analysis was able to offer a wide variety of information on the different aspects of respiratory infection and the immune response at the cellular level, including cell types, gene markers of interest, and expression patterns across different cell populations.  While the scope of this understanding may not be perfectly translatable to the human immune system from mouse models, it nonetheless proves most valuable in determining the most important areas of key research that can be explored, to further our knowledge of cell function, interaction, and response in health and disease application for humans.
